Electric operation

In 1952 Hodgkin and Huxley ([12], Figure 1) introduced the Nobel-prise winner idea of modeling neuron as an electric circuit (with current, potential, resistance and capacitance), although implemented using biological methods and materials. As they introduced, the neuron can be represented as a connected combination of resistance \(R\) and capacitance \(C\), i.e., a \(RC\) oscillator, a well-known electric element. In the case of biology it is implemented with a current drain (the AIS), (causing a voltage drop) and several current sources, 'native' (synaptic) and 'artificial' (patching and clamping) currents (causing a voltage raise). However, there are two possibilities to combine those elements, and those two combinations have drastically different behavior.

Neuronal membrane from the viewpoint of electronics

As discussed in [15], see also their Figure StructureOfAIS, the structure of the Axon Initial Segment is known to the smallest details [14]. It is known that "the axon initial segment (AIS) is located at the proximal axon and is the site of action potential initiation". Therefore describe AIS "as the action potential initiation site", which should be understood that AP is initiated by the neuron's membrane and the AIS mediates it to the axon. "After action potentials are generated, they propagate along axons to synaptic terminals" [15]. That is, from the point of view of electronics, we need to describe the output voltage that the circuit generates. For understanding how and why the AP is initiated, see the discussion below).

The structure of the Axon Initial Segment

As the illuminating investigations [19] in 2008 revealed, the AIS has very dense ion channels. That is, from electrical point of view, those parallelized conductances can be abstracted as a discrete conductance (or resistance) between the membrane and the axon, see Figure StructureOfAIS. The membrane itself, with ions on its surface, can be abstracted as a distributed condenser (in contrast with the viewpoint of biophysics, see PassiveNeuronalMembrane, no resistance). We need to be aware of the differences. There are no opposite charges on the two sides of the membrane (only the current of positive ions can be measured), membrane's capacitance should be interpreted differently: a slow current (represented by slow ions) is flowing in the direction of the potential gradient, and the ions are 'stored' only in the sense that they are inside the distributed capacitor while they traverse from their entry point to the AIS. The slow ions (representing the charge-up and synaptic entry currents, as well as the 'artificial currents' due to patching or clamping) need time to traverse from their entry point to the AIS. In this abstraction there is no place for an ideal conductor wiring. In some cases these differences matter and the classic neuronal membrane model commonly used in biology (shown in Figure PassiveNeuronalMembrane) is misleading (compare it to the circuits shown in Figure RCIntegratorCircuit ([18] Figure 1.1)). Notice that the figure suggests that resistance and capacitance are both discributed; there is no place for AIS as a separated (and discrete) element.

One of the most vital differences between those circuits (see also the figures in Table RCIntegratorCircuit) that their output is defined by the time integral of the input voltage (or current) or by its time derivative. From biological point of view, the differentiator can produce output voltage that differs from the input voltage in its sign, while the integrator cannot.

At the time of setting up electrical models for the neuron, the structure of neuron (see the top schematic in Figure StructureOfAIS), the AIS and its role in the electric operation was not known. Hodgkin and Huxley made a wrong choice of the circuit type, and their choice (probably by inertia) was repeated in good textbooks such as ([17] Figure 3.1) or ([18] Figure 1.1). The neuron is modeled as a passive RC integrator circuit where "the input is connected to a resistance while the output voltage is taken from across a capacitor". This wrong choice led to the need of assuming false ionic current and blocks understanding, among others, why AP is initiated. The mistake is expressed also in names such as "Integrate-and-fire" models and its derivatives. The right choice is a differentiator where "the input signal is applied to one side of the capacitor with the output taken across the resistor". From the discussion and figure above, it is clear that a neuron shall be modelled as a differentiator: the currents are directly created on the membrane (condenser) and the output voltage (AP) is taken across the resistor (AIS).

The Post-Synaptic Potential

Some schematic figures (for example See showing signal transmission from a presynaptic neuron to a postsynaptic neuron) miss the point that at the synapse the action potential appears as a different shape, furthermore mis-identifies the length of AP to essentially between the beginning of charge-up to the end of reaching the hyperpolarization peak voltage. Our discussion shows that the long tail after hyperpolarization is a vital part of AP (amond others, provides short-term neuronal memory for neuronal cooperation). Currents in different stages

The membrane's chargeup current (in our approximation) is an instant current: the ions rush in into the intracellular space in a very short time. After that, the ion current saturates at all locations over the membrane (in some sense, in direction 'perpendicularly' to membrane's surface). The membrane is an exceptionally good isolator, but at one end, at the AIS the ions can exit to the extracellular space. The resistance/conductance of AIS limits the current. The current outflow decreases the potential near to the outflow point and the ions from the higher potential will move towards the AIS. Two factors regulate the process. The first one is how quickly the ions can flow out through AIS: the current is proportional to the potential difference above resting potential. The second one is how quickly the ions can travel towards the potential gradient: the current is proportional to the local driving force.
