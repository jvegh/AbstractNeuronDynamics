Modeling ion channel

Recall that the drift speed and the thermal speed of ions differ by several orders of magnitude. The different speeds we have discussed play a significant role in the correct operation and the cooperation of different neuronal objects, including ion channels in the walls of membranes and axons. The massive difference in speeds explains why ion channel opening and closing resembles a 'digital operating mode'. They are either closed or open without a noticeable transition state. This behavior explains why ion currents start up with a sharp exponential rise [3] (fitting polynomial lines [12] to those critical regions was a big mistake: it hides the sudden change [3] caused by the state change of the ion channels); why initiating an AP has precise timings (both the charge-up signal and pressing ions through the AIS); why axonal arbors can provide a precise ‘Begin Computing’ signal. For the details, see the following subsections. Measuring the conductance of ion channels, requires special care. It is easy to make a systematic error, given that the measurement can affect the result.

When in intracellular fluid, a voltage-controlled ion channel in the wall of its section gets opened (for any reason), the ions in the immediate proximity of the channel have a great chance (a sizeable solid angle) to occupy a new, less crowded space by using their high thermal speed. How much they can use the possibility depends on the actual conditions, mainly concentrations and gradients. When they pass the channel, they face two effects on the two sides of the membrane. On the side of departure with high concentration, they suddenly ‘empty’ the thin layer in the immediate proximity of the membrane since the ions escape using their high thermal speed. Still, the ions in the next layer of the departure side can fill that thin ‘empty’ space only with their low drift speed. On the side of arrival with low concentration, again, the arrived ions suddenly form a ‘filled’ thin layer. However, following their arrival, they can use only their drift speed to populate the thin neighboring layer. (BTW: this effect can be misinterpreted as sudden ion adsorption [11] on the surface of the membrane.)

Simultaneously, the finite speed of ions is the reason why ion channels eventually close. On the one side, the rapid influx of ions is halted, causing a sudden increase in potential on the intracellular side. The significant concentration difference 'pushes' the ions into the intracellular space, where the potential on the inner side near the membrane's surface, which has suddenly increased, 'pushes back' the ions. Conversely, the ions' removal from the layer on the extracellular side near the membrane 'empties' the ions, and the after-diffusion (despite the large concentration difference) with the low drift speed takes time. Before the after-diffusion, the potential difference reverses and closes the ion channels.

Clamping sets up an artificial working regime for the ion channels: the permanent electric field on the outer surface keeps the ion channels open (in a stationary state) and enables ions to enter the inner surface where formerly no ions (and no potential) existed. The rushed-in ions will flow away from the place of clamping, and a slow current toward the membrane can start. As discussed in sectionTrue membrane current, when the membrane's potential reaches its threshold potential, after a similar rush-in over the membrane's surface, the ions can only leave through the AIS.
