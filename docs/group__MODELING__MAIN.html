Modeling abstract neurons

The operation of the CNS is closely related to information processing ('aka computing') of the brain. Neurons are one of the building stones of the living matter. Of course they do a lot of service activities to keep the cells active and healthy, to supply them with energy. However, for our goals their vital task is to receive, process and issue information, in cooperation with special assemblies of fellow neurons. Information and computing (in a general sense) play a fundamental role in understanding neuronal operation. When studying CNS, we need to start from cells to circuits [53], and we can study the brain 'from inside out' [12]. During this journey we need to keep in mind that we need to use (and with the growing complexity: change) the abstractions and approximations used to describe the abstract operation. Sometimes, even in describing the same phenomenon, different approximations must be used for the different participating interactions.

The goal was set [55] decades ago: 'The ultimate aim of computational neuroscience is to explain how electrical and chemical signals are used in the brain to represent and process information'. Since then, chemical and enzymatic research has advanced enormously. However, explaining the electrical signals, primarily how the brain uses them to represent and process information, has stalled, mainly because of using wrong approximations of biological reality. Biology uses ad-hoc mathematical models (typically lacking physical reality), and they deliver inconsistent, self-contradicting predictions, expectations, and interpretations for the real neurons and their networks. No place remains for interpreting the brain's neural information processing as a collective activity of neurons. Furthermore, we cannot discover that technical computing and the brain handle information differently [63].

However, the theoretical interpretation of the related phenomena is still missing. We make the first steps towards making an abstract understanding the phenomena. In the first chapter, at single-neuron level; in the second chapter at 'neurassembly' level (the collective operation). In the third chapter we present how the computing shall be generalized to describe also biological computing systems.
