Introducing a Novel Abstract Neuron(al) Theory and Simulator: A New Frontier in Neuroscience

The dynamic operation of individual neurons and their connections, higher-level organizations and their connections, and finally, the brain and the mind, are still among the big mysteries of science. At which point the non-living matter becomes a living one, which basic rules control the operation of neurons, how they cooperate in implementing more complex activity, what 'information' means for biological systems, and at which point a living matter becomes intelligent and conscious; whether and how science can handle all this stuff. The worst inheritances of neuroscience are the static view from anatomy, omitting revisiting periodically the basic hypotheses in the light of new research results; the abstractions of the classic science (the single speed, isolated pair-wise instant interactions) applied to biological matters without revisiting them; and applying ad-hoc mathematical formulas without correct physical processes in the background (actually, creating an alternative nature).

With its overwhelming complexity, nature presents a formidable challenge to scientific fields: we must use different approximations and abstractions. To accurately describe nature, we must navigate between 'Scylla' and 'Charybdis': maintaining sufficient accuracy and detail while managing the mathematical complexity, computational requirements, and possibilities. Currently, no theoretical description or simulator can adequately meet this challenge. The existing theoretical framework is based on an incorrect biophysical background and relies on outdated and partly incorrect empirical descriptions  from seven decades ago. Furthermore, the simulation uses principles and methods of time-unaware modeling and computing.

We attempt to see the forest for the trees. Nature uses an infinite variety of implementing neurons. However, in the CNS, they can cooperate. 'Despite the extraordinary diversity and complexity of neuronal morphology and synaptic connectivity, the nervous systems adopt a number of basic principles for all neurons and synapses' [33]. We base our discussion on those basic principles and create an 'abstract physical neuron', skipping the 'implementation details' nature uses. In many cases, wrong physical principles, notions, and measuring methods are used in measuring and modeling neurons; we need to discuss the true physics behind biological phenomena.

The 'neurons convey neural information by virtue of electrical and chemical signals' [33]. We agree that 'the fundamental task of the nervous system is to communicate and process information'. The goal was set decades ago: 'The ultimate aim of computational neuroscience is to explain how electrical and chemical signals are used in the brain to represent and process information' [55]. We need to understand how neurons represent and process information [63]. We agree that 'The basic structural units of the nervous system are individual neurons' [33], but we are also aware that neurons 'are linked together by dynamically changing constellations of synaptic weights' and 'cell assemblies are best understood in light of their output product' [11], so we also model multiple neurons.

'The brain computes! This statement is accepted as a truism by the majority of neuroscientists.' [34] However, 'we so far lack principles to understand rigorously how computation is done in living, or active, matter' [15]. To understand how "computation is done", we generalize computing [67] in close cooperation with communication [62]. We attempt to synthesize the available knowledge with a fresh eye and intend to leap into understanding neural computing, scrutinizing our knowledge pieces individually for credibility, relation to other pieces of other sciences, finding contradictions and resolutions, an defying fallacies. Sometimes, you may need our glossary or the search engine (see the right side of the title line).

Our site is not just about theory; we also provide a programmed implementation of the ideas we describe. Our simulator, based on direct science instead of ad-hoc mathematical formulas, is the only one capable of reproducing  the true biological time course of neurons from the first principles of science without ad-hoc assumptions and limited variability formulas. Our methods enable discussion of the major aspects from the phenomena of natural operation of neurons to analyzing the effects of invasive electricity-related investigation methods on neuroscience. We currently offer some demos, class implementations, and test cases to demonstrate our simulating capabilities. Our goal is to develop a comprehensive educational, demonstration, and research tool that will inspire and empower future generations of neuroscientists.
